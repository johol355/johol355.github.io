1 Predicting 30-day mortality

1.1 GLM with many predictors

In these models, many (but not all) predictors are included for prediction of 30-day mortality. These models should be interpreted with great caution. If the goal is to do causal inference, this is not the way to do it. If the goal is to build the strongest possible predictive model, a LASSO regression model should perhaps be used instead. However, I think there is zero interest in a predictive model using this population. Note that sending hospital is not included as a predictor here.

1.2 GLM with predictor choices made based on the DAG

Let’s define linear models with covariate choices based on the DAG. Now, how do you define severity of illness in a way that gives us the least biased estimate of the causal effect of transfer modality on mortality? Let’s think of the illness process as a latent process (i.e. the underlying patophysiological process) with partially observable manifestations that might, or might not, be present in our dataset. The important manifestations to include in the model are those that we belive might also influence the decision on transfer modality. Adding predictors that influence the mortality risk, but not the probability of “treatment” should (in the limit) increase the precision of the estimate. Adding more and more predictors comes with the risk of introducing spurious correlations such as post-treatment bias. An example of this would be the presence of assisted mechanical ventilation prior to transfer: if this is a result of the modality choice, a potential part of the causal effect on outcome will be blocked by including this as a predictor in the model.

Available predictors that seem reasonable to consider:

  • Diagnostic group (DX) DX influences outcome. And it seems reasonable that, when given the choice, the DX might influence our choice of transfer modality. DX contains much information about the complexity of the transfer and the expected in-transit interventions/complications. This might drive our decision to choose one modality over the other.

  • Consciousness level This is an imperfect measure of disease severity. But, in general, it is reflecting the severity of disease (the degree of ICP-elevation, the size of the mass lesion et. cetera). Also, consciousness level is in some circumstances explicitly used to make decisions on transfer modality. (Eg., in the place where I trained we had a protocol for the transfer of aSAH patients which “suggested” the use of a 2.5 hour ground-EMS transfer if the patient was GCS 14-15.)

  • SAPS3 score (Excluding the GCS component) This is a crude measure of physiological derrangements in ICU patients. Physiological derrangements are manifestations of a latent patophysiological state (such as hemorrhagic shock in a TBI patient)

  • Hypotension, hypoxia, hypothermia, acidosis et. cetera The decision making based on physiological measures such as these, is probably complex. My belief is that it is the “cumulative complexity” of the patient that might drive clinical decisions on transfers. While it is crude, it is at least partially captured by the SAPS3 score.

  • Mechanical ventilation I’m not sure that this will drive the decision on transfer modality choice. Also, I think that there is a small chance of post-treatment bias if this is included.

  • Demography (age, sex/gender) These predictors are connected with sending site, diagnosis and physiology in many interesting ways. However, do I think that they drive decisions on treatment assignment? Age, maybe. Sex, probably not. I don’t think it will hurt to include them though.

  • Sending site This predictor is important as it says a lot about the decision making process as well about the quality of care provided in the primary hospital. Also, since most sites primarily send their patients to only one of the university hospitals including sending site in the model also contains information about the care provided at the tertiary center. Personally, I think this one of the more important variables to include in the model.

  • Time Season and the time of day will without question drive the transfer modality choice. Will it affect outcome? Possibly. There is yearly variation in the prognosis of ICU patients and the reason for admission, this is in part captured by adjusting for DX and physiologic parameters. What is not captured is the effect of time on staffing and “healthcare system performance”. Therefore it seems reasonable to include time as a predictor. It can be included as categorical variables (12 for months and 24 for hours). An alternative is to divide the year into meaningful seasons (November-February: poor flying conditions, infection season. June-August: good flying conditions, staffing shortages due to vacations. September-october and March-April: intermediate flying conditions) and times of day (trickier since there is massive seasonal variation in the effect of time on hems minima). Introducing HEMS minima to the model will adjust partly for the seasonal/time effect on transfer modality choice. But this also carries with it a risk of post-treatment bias. You could for example choose to postpone the transfer for a couple of hours until minima are met… If this predictor is included, I think it would be wise to change the inclusion criteria of transfer within 24 h to transfer within 6 h (or maybe even 4 h) to decrease the risk of this bias.

  • Unobserved potentially observable covariates

    • More specific diagnostic information, eg. epidural hematoma vs rest of TBI group
    • Patient morphology (BMI/weight), >75% missing data
    • Early changes in GCS: a patient might regain conciousness (eg. seizure) and a low recorded GCS in the first hour might not reflect the disease process properly.
    • The list goes on for ever…

1.3 In part 2…

In part 2 we will explore models with more limited sets of covariates and multilevel models. Only Bayesian versions will be available as the frequentist models have no built-in missing data handling, tend not to converge and are hard to visualize with gtsummary. (Also it is very hard to write and test complicated models in a .qmd file with python code being fed to {reticulate}. Unfortunately, Positron has not figured out how to do this properly yet.)
